Shanghai

Where tradition meets the future

上海
Overview & History

China's Gateway to the World

A city of staggering contrasts — ancient temples sit in the shadow of supertall skyscrapers, and century-old laneway houses neighbour glittering shopping malls.

The Pearl of the Orient

From Fishing Village to Global Metropolis

Shanghai is China's largest city by population and its undisputed financial and commercial capital. Home to over 26 million people, it sits at the mouth of the Yangtze River on the eastern coast, making it one of the world's busiest port cities.

What began as a humble fishing village grew into a strategic trading post during the Song Dynasty. After the Treaty of Nanking in 1842, Shanghai opened to international trade and quickly became a cosmopolitan hub where European, American, and Asian cultures intermingled. The Bund — a waterfront promenade lined with grand colonial-era banks and trading houses — stands as a lasting monument to that era.

Across the Huangpu River, the Pudong district tells a completely different story. Barely farmland in the early 1990s, it is now home to the Shanghai Tower (632 metres), the Oriental Pearl TV Tower, and the Shanghai World Financial Centre — a futuristic skyline that rivals any on earth.

Weather & Scenery

When to Visit & What to See

Shanghai's subtropical climate means distinct seasons — plan your trip around the mild, pleasant months for the best experience.

Subtropical Monsoon Climate

Seasons, Scenery & Side Trips

Shanghai experiences a subtropical monsoon climate with four distinct seasons. Summers (June to August) are hot and humid, with temperatures regularly reaching 35 °C and occasional typhoon-season downpours. Winters (December to February) are chilly and damp, hovering around 3-8 °C, though snow is rare.

The best times to visit are spring (March to May) and autumn (September to November), when temperatures are mild (15-25 °C), rainfall is moderate, and the city's plane trees burst into fresh green or golden colour. Autumn also brings the famous hairy crab season — a culinary highlight you will not want to miss.

Beyond the city centre, Shanghai offers rewarding escapes into nature and heritage. The Huangpu River cruise provides stunning day and night views of the skyline. Zhujiajiao Water Town, just 50 kilometres west, is a 1,700-year-old canal village with stone bridges and traditional teahouses. Sheshan National Forest Park, south-west of the city, features hilltop walks, a basilica built by Jesuits in the 1860s, and an astronomical observatory.

Food & Cuisine

A Taste of Shanghai

Shanghai cuisine — known as Benbang cai — is characterised by its sweet, delicate flavours and masterful use of soy sauce, sugar, and Shaoxing rice wine.

Benbang Cai

Must-Try Shanghai Dishes

Shanghainese cooking favours braising, stewing, and steaming, resulting in rich yet refined dishes that let premium ingredients shine. The city's position at the coast and river delta means fresh seafood and river fish feature prominently alongside pork and poultry.

  • Xiaolongbao (soup dumplings) — Delicate steamed buns bursting with hot pork broth. Bite a small hole, sip the soup, then enjoy. Din Tai Fung and Jia Jia Tang Bao are local favourites.
  • Shengjianbao (pan-fried buns) — Crispy-bottomed, juicy pork buns with a golden sesame crust. A beloved breakfast street food found at Yang's Fried Dumplings across the city.
  • Hairy crab (seasonal, Oct-Dec) — Prized for its rich golden roe, steamed and eaten with a black vinegar and ginger dip. A seasonal delicacy worth planning your trip around.
  • Hong shao rou (red-braised pork belly) — Meltingly tender pork belly slow-cooked in soy sauce, sugar, and Shaoxing wine until caramelised and glossy.
  • Cong you ban mian (scallion oil noodles) — A deceptively simple dish of thin noodles tossed in fragrant caramelised shallot oil and soy sauce. Deeply savoury and addictive.

Things to Do

Cultural Activities & Experiences

Shanghai offers a rich blend of historic landmarks, vibrant neighbourhoods, and unique cultural experiences that make every day unforgettable.

🏯

Yu Garden & Old City God Temple

Explore this exquisite classical Chinese garden dating back to the Ming Dynasty (1559). Wander through pavilions, rockeries, and koi ponds, then browse the bustling bazaar surrounding the City God Temple for souvenirs, snacks, and traditional crafts.

🌃

The Bund Waterfront Walk

Stroll along the iconic 1.5-kilometre promenade on the west bank of the Huangpu River. Admire 52 heritage buildings in Art Deco, Baroque, and Neoclassical styles on one side, with Pudong's futuristic skyline glittering across the water. Magical at night.

🌳

French Concession

Lose yourself in tree-lined avenues flanked by charming 1920s villas, independent boutiques, artisan coffee shops, and hidden cocktail bars. The streets around Wukang Road and Fuxing Park are perfect for a leisurely afternoon of wandering and people-watching.

🛍️

Nanjing Road Shopping Street

Walk China's most famous pedestrian shopping street stretching over five kilometres from the Bund to Jing'an Temple. From heritage department stores to flagship global brands and lively street performers, this boulevard pulses with energy day and night.

🎭

Acrobatic & Teahouse Shows

Catch a jaw-dropping Shanghai acrobatic show at the Shanghai Centre Theatre or ERA Intersection of Time — a world-class spectacle of agility and artistry. For something calmer, visit the Huxinting Teahouse in Yu Garden for traditional tea served over a zigzag bridge.

⛵

Zhujiajiao Water Town Day Trip

Just an hour from central Shanghai, this 1,700-year-old water town offers gondola rides through willow-draped canals, ancient stone bridges, and local rice wine tasting. A peaceful escape that feels a world away from the city's skyscrapers.
