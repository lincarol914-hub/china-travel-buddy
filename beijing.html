Beijing

The heart of Chinese civilisation

Overview & History

An Imperial Capital for Over 800 Years

Beijing has served as the political and cultural centre of China since the Yuan Dynasty established it as the imperial capital in 1271. Today it is home to more than 21 million people and remains the seat of the Chinese government, a powerhouse of art and academia, and a living museum of the nation's history.

The city boasts six UNESCO World Heritage Sites, including the Forbidden City — the world's largest palace complex — and the magnificent Temple of Heaven. Walk through Tiananmen Square, one of the largest public squares on Earth, then lose yourself in the ancient hutong neighbourhoods where narrow alleyways wind between traditional courtyard houses that have stood for centuries.

Weather & Natural Scenery

Four Distinct Seasons, One Golden Autumn

Beijing has a continental monsoon climate with four sharply defined seasons. The best time to visit is September through October, when golden autumn brings clear blue skies, comfortable temperatures around 15-25 °C, and the city's parks erupt in brilliant shades of red and gold.

Spring (March to May) can be windy and occasionally dusty, though the blossoming cherry trees in Yuyuantan Park make it worthwhile. Summers are hot and humid, with temperatures regularly exceeding 35 °C. Winters are cold and dry, often dipping below -5 °C, but offer the unique experience of seeing the Great Wall dusted in snow with far fewer crowds.

  • Hike the Great Wall at Mutianyu for restored sections with stunning mountain views, or try the wilder, less-crowded Jinshanling stretch
  • Explore the Western Hills (Xishan) for peaceful hiking trails and centuries-old Buddhist temples
  • Visit the Ming Tombs, the imperial burial ground set against a dramatic mountain backdrop
  • Stroll through the Summer Palace gardens beside Kunming Lake, especially beautiful in autumn

Food & Cuisine

Imperial Traditions Meet Street Food Culture

Beijing's cuisine reflects centuries of imperial court influence blended with hearty northern Chinese flavours. Rich, savoury, and deeply satisfying, the food here ranges from world-famous roast duck to humble breakfast crepes sold on every street corner.

  • Peking Duck — The city's most iconic dish. Crispy, lacquered skin carved tableside and wrapped in thin pancakes with hoisin sauce, cucumber, and spring onion. Book ahead at renowned spots like Quanjude or Da Dong.
  • Zhajiangmian — Thick hand-pulled noodles topped with a rich fermented soybean paste sauce, julienned cucumber, and radish. A beloved Beijing comfort food.
  • Jianbing — A savoury breakfast crepe made fresh on a griddle with egg, crispy wonton cracker, coriander, and chilli sauce. The ultimate grab-and-go morning meal.
  • Lamb Hotpot (Shuan Yangrou) — Thinly sliced lamb swished through boiling copper-pot broth with sesame dipping sauce. A warming Beijing winter tradition.
  • Beijing Yoghurt — Sweet, drinkable yoghurt served in small ceramic jars. A refreshing treat sold at street stalls across the city.
  • Tanghulu — Skewered hawthorn berries dipped in a crackling sugar glaze. A nostalgic Beijing street snack that photographs beautifully.

Cultural Activities

Experiences You Won't Find Anywhere Else

Beijing offers a remarkable blend of ancient traditions and contemporary culture. Whether you are drawn to imperial history, modern art, or local neighbourhood life, there is something here that will stay with you long after you leave.

  • Hutong exploration — Wander through traditional hutong neighbourhoods by rickshaw or on foot. These narrow alleyways reveal courtyard homes, tiny tea houses, and a quieter side of Beijing that has endured for generations.
  • Temple of Heaven — Visit this stunning 15th-century temple complex and watch locals practicing tai chi, dancing, and playing traditional instruments in the surrounding park every morning.
  • 798 Art District — A converted military factory complex now housing galleries, studios, and cafes showcasing the best of contemporary Chinese art and design.
  • Peking Opera — Attend a performance of this 200-year-old art form featuring elaborate costumes, acrobatic movement, and distinctive vocal techniques. The Liyuan Theatre offers tourist-friendly shows with English subtitles.
  • Climb the Great Wall — One of the New Seven Wonders of the World. The Mutianyu section offers a cable car, toboggan ride down, and beautifully restored watchtowers surrounded by forested mountains.
  • Panjiayuan Antique Market — Browse one of Asia's largest flea markets for unique souvenirs, including vintage propaganda posters, jade carvings, calligraphy brushes, and handmade crafts.
