How to Buy Train Tickets in China as a Foreigner — Complete 2026 Guide

April 13, 20269 min readChina Travel Buddy

China’s high-speed rail network is one of the modern wonders of travel. With over 45,000 kilometres of track connecting virtually every major city, it is faster than flying for most domestic routes once you factor in airport wait times. A Beijing-to-Shanghai trip takes just four and a half hours, and tickets can cost as little as 550 yuan (around 60 GBP) for a second-class seat.

The problem? Buying train tickets as a foreigner in China is not as straightforward as it should be. The booking systems were designed primarily for Chinese ID card holders, and passport users face extra steps. This guide walks you through every method available in 2026, from apps to station counters, so you can travel by rail with confidence.

Understanding China’s Train Types

Before you book, it helps to know what you are booking. China operates several categories of train, and the differences in speed, comfort, and price are significant.

Train TypeSpeedComfortBest For
G-train (Gaotie)Up to 350 km/hExcellent — clean, spacious, quietMajor city connections (Beijing-Shanghai, Guangzhou-Shenzhen)
D-train (Dongche)Up to 250 km/hVery goodRegional routes and slightly cheaper alternatives
C-train (Chengji)Up to 200 km/hGoodShort intercity routes
K/T/Z-trains80-120 km/hBasic — older rolling stockBudget overnight journeys and remote destinations

For most foreign visitors, G-trains and D-trains are the ones you will use. They are fast, comfortable, run frequently, and cover all major tourist destinations.

Seat Classes Explained

High-speed trains offer multiple seat classes. Here is what to expect:

  • Second Class (二等座): The standard and most popular option. Comfortable reclining seats in a 3+2 configuration. Plenty of legroom compared to economy flights.
  • First Class (一等座): Wider seats in a 2+2 layout. More recline, more space, quieter carriages. Typically 40-60% more expensive than second class.
  • Business Class (商务座): Available on some G-trains. Fully reclining leather seats, complimentary snacks. Roughly double the price of first class.
  • Standing Ticket (无座): Same price as second class but no guaranteed seat. Only recommended as an absolute last resort.
Our Recommendation

Second class is excellent value and perfectly comfortable for journeys up to five hours. For longer trips or if you want a quieter ride, first class is worth the upgrade.

Method 1: Booking via Trip.com (Easiest for Foreigners)

Trip.com (formerly Ctrip’s international platform) is the most foreigner-friendly way to book Chinese train tickets. The app and website are fully in English, accept international credit cards, and handle the passport-number complications for you.

  1. 1
    Download the Trip.com app or visit trip.comCreate an account using your email. No Chinese phone number required.
  2. 2
    Search for your routeEnter your departure city, destination city, and travel date. The app shows all available trains with times, duration, and prices.
  3. 3
    Select your train and seat classTap on your preferred departure time. Choose between second class, first class, or business class.
  4. 4
    Enter your passport detailsYou must enter your full name exactly as it appears on your passport and your passport number.
  5. 5
    Pay with your international cardTrip.com accepts Visa, Mastercard, and most international payment methods. You will receive an e-confirmation instantly.
Important: Ticket Collection

After booking through Trip.com, you may need to collect a physical ticket at the station, or in many cases your passport now works as your ticket at automated gates.

Method 2: Booking via the Official 12306 App

The China Railway 12306 app is the official government booking platform. It offers the widest selection of tickets and releases them first (usually 15 days before departure). However, it is more challenging for foreigners to use.

Setting Up a 12306 Account with a Passport

  • Download the 12306 app from the App Store or Google Play.
  • Select “passport” as your ID type during registration.
  • Enter your passport number and full name exactly as they appear on your passport.
  • You will need a Chinese phone number for SMS verification. Consider getting a Chinese SIM or eSIM first.
  • Identity verification may require visiting a station ticket window with your passport, or try in-app facial recognition.

Once set up, 12306 works well and prices are the official rates with no service fees.

Method 3: Booking via Alipay Mini-Programs

If you already have Alipay set up (see our Alipay setup guide), you can book train tickets directly through Alipay’s built-in mini-programs without needing a separate app.

Open Alipay, search for “train tickets” or “12306” in the search bar, and you will find the official railway mini-program.

Method 4: Buying at the Station

You can always buy tickets at the train station itself. Every major station has both automated ticket machines and staffed ticket windows.

At Ticket Machines

Most modern stations have self-service machines that accept passport scans. Look for machines with an English language option. Place your passport on the scanner, select your route, choose your train, and pay.

At Ticket Windows

Staffed ticket counters accept passports. Prepare by having your destination, date, and preferred time written in Chinese characters on your phone.

Pro Tip: Peak Travel Warning

During Chinese public holidays — especially Spring Festival (late January/February), National Day (October 1-7), and Labour Day (May 1-5) — tickets sell out within minutes of release. Book exactly 15 days in advance at 8:00 AM China time.

Getting Through the Station: What to Expect

Chinese train stations operate more like airports than the open-platform stations common in Europe. Here is the process:

  1. 1
    Arrive earlyPlan to arrive 30-45 minutes before departure. Large stations like Beijing South or Shanghai Hongqiao can take 15 minutes just to navigate.
  2. 2
    Security screeningAll bags go through an X-ray scanner at the station entrance.
  3. 3
    ID and ticket checkPresent your passport at the entry gates. Many stations now support passport scanning at automated gates.
  4. 4
    Find your waiting hallCheck the departure boards for your train number and corresponding waiting hall.
  5. 5
    Board when gates openBoarding typically begins 15-20 minutes before departure. Your ticket shows your carriage and seat number.

Essential Tips for Foreigners Riding Chinese Trains

  • Passport name matching is strict. Your booking name must match your passport exactly.
  • Tickets are non-transferable. Every ticket is linked to your passport number.
  • Refunds are possible but have fees. Cancelling more than 48 hours before departure incurs a 5% fee. Within 24 hours, 15-20%.
  • Food and drinks are available on board. Trolley service passes through carriages regularly.
  • Wi-Fi is limited. Some newer G-trains offer Wi-Fi, but it is unreliable. Download offline maps before your journey.
  • Power outlets are available. Most high-speed trains have power sockets under or between seats.
  • Luggage space is generous. Overhead racks are large. There are no strict luggage limits on Chinese trains.

Final Thoughts: Trains Are the Best Way to See China

China’s rail network is fast, affordable, and connects virtually every destination a tourist would want to visit. The booking process has its quirks for passport holders, but with Trip.com as a reliable backup and the 12306 app improving for foreign users, getting tickets is entirely manageable.

Our advice: book your major intercity journeys at least a week in advance, especially during busy seasons. Use Trip.com for simplicity or 12306 for the best prices and earliest ticket access. And enjoy the ride — watching the Chinese countryside fly past at 350 km/h is an experience in itself.
